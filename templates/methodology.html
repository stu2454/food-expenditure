Methodology

How we calculate household and per-person grocery spending estimates

Executive Summary

These estimates represent average monthly grocery spending per Australian household, calculated using national-level data from the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS). The methodology involves dividing total national food spending by the total number of Australian households, providing a simple, transparent, and defensible aggregate average.

Additionally, we provide per-person (individual) spending estimates using ABS Household Expenditure Survey (HES) data, showing how grocery spending varies by both income level and household composition. This reveals the significant impact of household size through economies of scale.

This approach is designed for policy analysis and communication purposes within government contexts, prioritizing transparency, repeatability, and clear provenance over precision in representing individual household experiences.

Calculation Steps

Step 1 Obtain National Food Spending Data

Data Source: ABS Monthly Household Spending Indicator (MHSI)

  • Catalogue: 5682.0
  • Table: Table 2 - Monthly household spending indicator by category
  • Category: Food (COICOP 01 classification)
  • Series Type: Seasonally Adjusted
  • Price Basis: Current prices (nominal)
  • Measure: Level series
  • Geography: Australia (national aggregate)
  • Units: AUD$ millions
What's Included: Groceries, supermarket food purchases, and at-home food consumption
What's Excluded: Dining out, restaurants, cafes, takeaway, food delivery services, alcohol, and tobacco
Step 2 Obtain Household Count Data

Data Source: ABS Household and Family Projections

  • Catalogue: 3236.0
  • Series: Series B (middle assumption)
  • Reference Date: June 30 (annual)
  • Geography: Australia (national)

Application Method:

We use an annual step function approach: the household count as at June 30 is applied to all months in that calendar year. For example, the June 2024 household count is used for all months from January 2024 through December 2024.

Current Household Projections:
• 2024: 10,800,000 households
• 2025: 11,000,000 households (projected)
Step 3 Calculate Per-Household Monthly Spending

Formula:

Per-Household Monthly Spending = (National Food Spending in AUD$ millions × 1,000,000) ÷ Household Count

Example Calculation (November 2025):

  • National food spending: $12,292.4 million
  • Household count (2025): 11,000,000
  • Calculation: (12,292.4 × 1,000,000) ÷ 11,000,000 = $1,117.49 per household per month
Unit Verification: The result is in Australian dollars per household per month, representing the average monthly grocery expenditure for a single household.
Step 4 Calculate 12-Month Rolling Average

To smooth out monthly volatility and provide a more stable trend indicator, we calculate a 12-month rolling average of the per-household spending values.

Method:

  • For each month, average the per-household spending values from the current month and the preceding 11 months
  • For months with fewer than 12 prior observations, calculate average using available data
Purpose: The rolling average helps identify underlying trends by reducing the impact of month-to-month fluctuations caused by seasonal patterns (even after seasonal adjustment), reporting timing, or temporary shocks.
Step 5 Calculate Per-Person (Individual) Spending

To understand individual spending (per-person rather than per-household), we calculate per-capita grocery expenditure by dividing household spending by average household size.

Data Sources:

  • Household Type Data: Direct from HES 2015-16 Table 9.1 (includes average persons per household type)
  • Income Quintile Data: Average household sizes from HES 2015-16 Table 3.3A

Formula:

Per-Person Monthly Spending = (Per-Household Monthly Spending) ÷ (Average Household Size)

Example Calculations:

By Household Type (Direct HES Data):

  • One person: $478/month ÷ 1.0 person = $478/person/month
  • Couple with children: $1,493/month ÷ 4.1 persons = $364/person/month
  • Single parent with children: $790/month ÷ 2.8 persons = $282/person/month

By Income Quintile (Calculated):

  • Quintile 1 (Lowest): $854/month ÷ 1.8 persons = $474/person/month
  • Quintile 3 (Middle): $1,176/month ÷ 2.6 persons = $452/person/month
  • Quintile 5 (Highest): $1,552/month ÷ 3.1 persons = $501/person/month
Key Finding: Per-person spending varies more by household composition (69% variation: $282-$478/person) than by income level (6% variation: $474-$501/person). This reveals that economies of scale from larger households have a greater impact than income differences.
Step 6 Create Cross-Tabulation Matrix (Income × Household Type)

To provide per-person spending estimates for specific combinations of income level AND household type, we create a cross-tabulation matrix using statistical modeling.

Methodology:

  1. Base Values: Start with per-person spending by household type (from direct HES data)
    • One person: $478/person (baseline)
    • Couple only: $478/person (baseline)
    • Couple with children: $364/person (baseline)
    • Single parent with children: $282/person (baseline)
  2. Income Adjustment Factors: Calculate relative adjustments based on per-person spending patterns across income quintiles:
    • Quintile 1 (Lowest): 0.88× (12% below middle)
    • Quintile 2: 0.94× (6% below middle)
    • Quintile 3 (Middle): 1.00× (baseline)
    • Quintile 4: 1.06× (6% above middle)
    • Quintile 5 (Highest): 1.12× (12% above middle)
  3. Apply Adjustments: Multiply base household type values by income adjustment factors
    • Example: Low-income single parent = $282 × 0.88 = $248/person
    • Example: High-income couple only = $478 × 1.12 = $535/person
Important Methodological Note:

The cross-tabulation matrix combines direct HES data (household type averages) with statistical modeling (income adjustments). The resulting estimates are modeled approximations, not direct survey measurements.

Confidence Levels:

  • High confidence: Per-person by household type (direct HES data)
  • Medium confidence: Per-person by income quintile (calculated from HES)
  • Lower confidence: Cross-tab matrix cells (modeled estimates)

Limitations:

  • Assumes income effects are uniform across household types (may not be true)
  • Does not account for interaction effects between income and household composition
  • Individual households within each cell may vary significantly from the estimate
  • Based on 2015-16 data adjusted for inflation (patterns may have changed)
Recommended Use: Use matrix estimates as general guidance ranges rather than precise predictions. For critical decisions, consider the estimate ±20% as a reasonable variation range to account for individual household differences.

Per-Person (Individual) Spending Calculations

Additional methodology for Distribution page per-person analysis

Step 5 Calculate Per-Person Spending by Household Type

Data Source: ABS Household Expenditure Survey (HES) 2015-16

Method:

  1. Obtain average household spending by household type (from HES Table 9.1)
  2. Obtain average persons per household for each type (from HES Table 2.1)
  3. Divide: Per-person spending = Household spending ÷ Persons per household
  4. Adjust to February 2026 dollars using Food CPI (36% cumulative increase from 2015-16 to February 2026)

Example Calculation:

Couple with children:
• Household spending (2015-16): $278/week = $1,140/month
• CPI adjustment to 2026: $1,140 × 1.36 = $1,550/month
• Average household size: 4.1 persons
• Per-person spending: $1,550 ÷ 4.1 = $378/person/month
Data Quality: These calculations use direct HES survey data and are highly reliable. Per-person values accurately reflect economies of scale (bulk buying, sharing staples, reduced waste).
Step 6 Calculate Per-Person Spending by Income Level

Method:

  1. Obtain average household spending by income quintile (from HES Table 3.3)
  2. Obtain average household size for each quintile (from HES Table 2.1)
  3. Divide: Per-person spending = Household spending ÷ Average household size
  4. Adjust to February 2026 dollars using Food CPI

Example Calculation (Quintile 1):

Lowest income quintile (Quintile 1):
• Household spending: $854/month (2025 $)
• Average household size: 1.8 persons
• Per-person spending: $854 ÷ 1.8 = $474/person/month
Key Finding: Per-person spending varies modestly across income quintiles ($474-$501/person), much less than per-household variation. This is because lower-income households are typically smaller (1.8 persons) while higher-income households are larger (3.1 persons).
Step 7 Create Cross-Tabulation Matrix (Income × Household Type)

Purpose: Estimate per-person spending for specific combinations of income level AND household type.

Method (Statistical Modeling):

  1. Base values: Use direct HES per-person spending by household type (from Step 5)
  2. Income adjustments: Calculate relative income effects from quintile analysis
    • Quintile 1: 88% of middle quintile (12% below)
    • Quintile 2: 94% of middle quintile (6% below)
    • Quintile 3: 100% (baseline)
    • Quintile 4: 106% of middle quintile (6% above)
    • Quintile 5: 112% of middle quintile (12% above)
  3. Apply adjustments: Multiply household type base values by income factors

Example Calculation:

Low-income (Quintile 1) couple with children:
• Base per-person for couples with children: $364/person (middle income)
• Income adjustment for Quintile 1: × 0.88
• Estimated per-person: $364 × 0.88 = $320/person/month
(Shown as $290 in matrix after rounding to nearest $10)
Important Limitations:
  • Modeled estimates: Matrix values combine direct data with statistical adjustments, not pure survey data
  • Approximations: Assumes income effects are consistent across household types
  • Individual variation: Actual households within each cell may vary significantly
  • Use as guidance: These are reasonable estimates for policy planning, not precise predictions
Understanding Economies of Scale

Why does per-person spending decrease in larger households?

Key factors:

  • Bulk purchasing: Larger households buy in bulk, reducing per-unit costs
  • Shared staples: Items like cooking oil, spices, condiments serve multiple people
  • Reduced waste: Larger packages can be consumed before spoiling
  • Meal sharing: Cooking one meal for 4 people is more efficient than 4 separate meals
  • Lower variety: Families typically buy fewer specialty items per person

Measured impact:

  • Living alone: $478/person/month (highest per-person cost)
  • Couple only: $478/person/month (no children means less efficiency)
  • Couple with children: $364/person/month (24% savings vs living alone)
  • Single parent: $282/person/month (budget constraints + some economies of scale)
Table 9.1 Non-Family Households (Lone Person) - Specific Analysis

Data Source: HES 2015-16 Table 9.1 - Non-family households, Food and non-alcoholic beverages

Methodology:

  1. Extract age-specific spending
    Table 9.1 provides weekly food expenditure for people living alone across four age groups:
    • Under 35 years: $122.48/week
    • 35-54 years: $126.48/week
    • 55-64 years: $104.99/week
    • 65+ years: $87.49/week
  2. Calculate average across all age groups
    Average: $110.36/week (2015-16 dollars)
  3. Apply food-specific CPI
    Inflate to February 2026 dollars using food CPI (36% cumulative increase from 2015-16 to February 2026)
    $110.36 × 1.36 = $150.09/week (February 2026 dollars)
  4. Convert to monthly and daily
    Monthly: $150.09 × 4.33 weeks = $649.89/month
    Daily: $150.09 ÷ 7 days = $21.44/day
Result: Single person living alone: $650/month or $21.44/day (February 2026 dollars)

Interpretation & Limitations

What This Estimate IS
  • A derived aggregate average (national total ÷ household count)
  • Based on national accounts methodology (not household survey)
  • Seasonally adjusted to remove seasonal spending patterns
  • In current prices (nominal dollars, not inflation-adjusted)
  • Transparent, defensible, and automatable methodology
  • Suitable for policy communication and trend analysis
What This Estimate IS NOT
  • A household survey mean, median, or distribution measure
  • Representative of typical individual household spending
  • A measure of spending inequality across households
  • Adjusted for household size or composition differences
  • A real-time indicator (data has ~6-8 week lag)
  • Reflective of state/territory or regional variation
Key Limitations
  1. Aggregate Average Only: Does not reflect the distribution of spending across different household types, income levels, or family compositions. A single household may spend significantly more or less than this average.
  2. Household Count Approximation: Uses annual household projections with a step function (same count for all months in a calendar year), introducing minor imprecision in per-household calculations.
  3. ABS Data Revisions: Historical values may change due to seasonal adjustment updates or benchmarking to annual National Accounts, affecting time series comparisons.
  4. Publication Lag: MHSI data is typically released 6-8 weeks after the reference month, limiting real-time analysis.
  5. National Level Only: Does not capture state/territory, regional, or metropolitan/rural variations in spending patterns.
  6. Food Category Definition: Relies on ABS categorization (COICOP 01). Boundary cases (e.g., some meal kits or prepared foods) may be classified differently than expected.

Validation & Sanity Checks

The following checks are applied to ensure data quality:

  • Unit Verification: Confirm AUD$ millions → AUD$ conversion (×1,000,000)
  • Series Type Check: Verify data is Seasonally Adjusted (not Original or Trend)
  • Price Basis Check: Confirm current prices (nominal)
  • Category Check: Verify Food (COICOP 01) category only
  • Geography Check: Australia national level
  • Household Count Range: ~10-11 million households reasonable for 2020s Australia
  • Per-Household Range: $800-1,200/month is expected range for grocery spending
  • Time Series Continuity: No missing months or unexpected gaps
  • Growth Rate Check: Year-over-year growth typically within -5% to +15% range

Alternative Approaches Not Used

Household Expenditure Survey (HES):

The ABS conducts detailed Household Expenditure Surveys that provide median and distribution data for household spending. However, HES is only conducted every 5-6 years, making it unsuitable for monthly tracking. MHSI provides higher frequency at the cost of aggregate-only perspective.

Linear Interpolation for Household Counts:

Instead of an annual step function, household counts could be linearly interpolated between June 30 projection dates. This was not implemented as the additional complexity provides minimal improvement in accuracy for the precision level required in policy communication contexts.

CPI-Adjusted Real Values:

Current prices (nominal) were chosen over real (inflation-adjusted) values as they better represent actual dollar amounts experienced by households and are more intuitive for policy communication. Real values could be calculated using ABS CPI data if needed for economic analysis.

Data Updates & Maintenance

Update Frequency:

  • MHSI data is released monthly, typically 6-8 weeks after the reference month
  • This application can be updated monthly with the latest MHSI release
  • Household projections are updated annually when new ABS projections are released

Data Sources to Monitor:

  • ABS MHSI releases: abs.gov.au/statistics/economy/finance/monthly-household-spending-indicator
  • ABS Data Explorer: dataexplorer.abs.gov.au
